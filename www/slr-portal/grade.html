GRADE Methodology Overview

Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE) is a systematic framework for assessing evidence quality across 5 domains. Each paper rated on Risk of Bias, Inconsistency, Indirectness, Imprecision, and Publication Bias.

Quality Levels

GRADE A
High Quality: Further research unlikely to change effect estimate. RCTs without serious limitations or consistent cohort studies.
GRADE B
Moderate Quality: Further research may change estimate. RCT with limitations, cohort studies, or inconsistent results.
GRADE C
Low Quality: Further research very likely to change estimate. Case-control or observational studies with limitations.
GRADE D
Very Low Quality: Any estimate very uncertain. Case reports, expert opinion, or severe methodological flaws.

Assessment Domains

  • Risk of Bias: Study design quality, confounding control, attrition bias
  • Inconsistency: Homogeneity of results across studies, heterogeneity (I²)
  • Indirectness: Relevance to target population, outcomes, and setting
  • Imprecision: Wide confidence intervals, small sample sizes, adequate power
  • Publication Bias: Funnel plot asymmetry, selective outcome reporting

📈 Metabolic Syndrome Quality Assessment (3 Papers)

MET-001: IDF Definition 2019
IDF Consensus Group (2019)
GRADE A HIGH Certainty

Assessment: International consensus definition. Synthesizes epidemiological evidence for 5-component diagnostic criteria. 3,900+ citations indicate universal acceptance. MetS definition links to increased CVD (2-4x) and diabetes (5-9x) risk. Applicable across populations.

MET-002: MetS & CVD Risk Meta-Analysis
Mottillo S, et al. (2010) | JACC
GRADE A HIGH Certainty

Assessment: Systematic meta-analysis of 87 studies (900,000+ subjects). Quantifies CVD risk increase (RR 2.35, 95% CI 2.02-2.74) and all-cause mortality (1.52x). Large sample, multiple independent studies, consistent findings. 1,650+ citations. Robust evidence for MetS as CVD risk amplifier.

MET-003: PREDIMED Mediterranean Diet RCT
Estruch R, et al. (2013) | NEJM
GRADE A HIGH Certainty

Assessment: Multicenter RCT (7,447 subjects, 4.8-year follow-up). Demonstrates 30% CVD event reduction with Mediterranean diet in MetS/high-risk. Low risk of bias, adequate blinding, primary prevention setting. 2,800+ citations. Strong evidence for dietary intervention efficacy.

Metabolic Syndrome Evidence Summary

Overall Grade: A | Papers: 3 | Quality: All Grade A | MetS diagnostic criteria, CVD risk quantification, and dietary prevention strategies supported by consensus, meta-analysis, and RCT evidence.
